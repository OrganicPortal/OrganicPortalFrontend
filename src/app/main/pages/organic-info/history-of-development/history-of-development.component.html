Development history

The founder of the concept of organic farming as a form of agriculture is considered to be the Japanese philosopher Mokishi Okada (1882-1955). He believed that ecological agricultural technology should produce food that supports life and improves people's health; stabilizes the biological balance in nature and is environmentally safe.

In Europe, organic production began to gain popularity from the middle of the 20th century. The famous Austrian philosopher Rudolf Steiner (1861 - 1925) created the theory of anthroposophy, according to which a person should feel like a part of the Universe and strive to live in harmony with the world around him, maintaining a balance between the moral and the material. Towards the end of his life, he developed the concept of "biodynamic agriculture". Within the framework of this concept, the farmer became the keeper of balance - healthy animals needed to be fed by healthy plants, healthy plants depended on healthy soil, and healthy soil - on healthy animals.

The term "organic" was first used in 1940. It appeared in the book "Relying on the Land" by the British author Walter Northbourne, who believed that each farm should be a single organism with a balanced and fulfilling life, where living things and plants should be in harmony with each other. 

 In the 1970s in France, the International Federation of Organic Agriculture Movements (IFOAM) was founded.  The aim of this organization was to define the "role of organic agriculture in production, processing, distribution or consumption to maintain and improve the health of ecosystems and living beings from soil microorganisms to humans.  The Research Institute for Organic Agriculture (FiBL, Switzerland) was founded. To this day, it remains one of the world's leading research and information centers for organic agriculture. 

In Ukraine, the first organic farm is considered to be the private enterprise "Agroekologiya" (Poltava region), which over 30 years ago, under the leadership of Antonets S.S., completely abandoned the use of herbicides, pesticides, and mineral fertilizers, and switched to organic production methods.
